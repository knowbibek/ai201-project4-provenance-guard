# Provenance Guard — Planning

> Design document. Filled in **before** implementation (Milestone 1). Implementation lives in the
> source files documented here; user-facing evidence (label text, rate limits, audit log) is
> mirrored into the README, which is the canonical record graders rely on.

## Problem & Goals

Creative platforms (writing, music, art) need a way to tell readers whether a submitted piece was
likely written by a human or generated by AI — **to protect attribution and give context, not to
police creativity.** Provenance Guard is a backend any such platform can plug into. It:

1. Classifies submitted text (human / AI / uncertain),
2. Scores confidence honestly instead of forcing a binary,
3. Surfaces a plain-language transparency label, and
4. Lets creators appeal a classification.

**Core design bias — false positives are the worst outcome.** Wrongly labeling a human's work as
AI ("you cheated") does real reputational harm and is unfair; wrongly missing some AI is a softer
failure. Everywhere there is a tradeoff, the system tilts toward *not* accusing a human: a higher
evidence bar to say "AI," and an honest "uncertain" verdict whenever the signals don't strongly agree.

## Architecture Narrative (the path one piece of text takes)

A creator submits a piece of text. Here is its full journey from request to the label a reader sees,
naming every component it touches:

1. **HTTP request → `POST /submit`** (Flask route). The platform sends the raw text (plus optional
   `creator_id`/`title`). This is the entry point and request validator — it rejects empty or
   oversized input.

2. **Rate Limiter (Flask-Limiter).** Before any work happens, the request is checked against
   per-client limits. If the client is over budget, it's rejected with `429` and the work below
   never runs. This protects the (cost-bearing) Groq calls from flooding/abuse.

3. **Detection Pipeline — Signal 1: LLM classifier (Groq, `llama-3.3-70b-versatile`).** The raw text
   is sent to the model with a structured prompt asking it to judge human-vs-AI and return a
   JSON-shaped `ai_likelihood` ∈ [0,1] plus a short rationale. This captures *semantic / stylistic*
   coherence holistically.

4. **Detection Pipeline — Signal 2: Stylometry (pure Python).** The same raw text is run through a
   set of statistical heuristics (sentence-length variance, lexical diversity, punctuation density).
   These are mapped to their own `ai_likelihood` ∈ [0,1]. This captures *structural* properties — no
   network, no model.

5. **Confidence Fusion.** The two signal scores are combined into one `ai_likelihood` (weighted) and
   one `confidence` value. Crucially, **signal disagreement lowers confidence** and pulls the verdict
   toward "uncertain." The asymmetric thresholds (higher bar for "AI") are applied here.

6. **Label Builder.** The `(verdict, confidence)` pair selects one of three transparency-label
   variants and produces the exact reader-facing text.

7. **Audit Log (SQLite).** The full decision — text hash, both raw signal scores, combined score,
   confidence, verdict, label variant, timestamp — is written as one structured row. This is the
   permanent record and the source for `GET /log`.

8. **HTTP response.** A structured JSON object (submission id, verdict, scores, confidence, the label
   text, and the per-signal breakdown) is returned to the platform, which shows the label to readers.

**Appeal path:** later, a creator who disputes the verdict calls `POST /appeal` with their reasoning.
The system looks up the original decision, writes an **appeal row** to the audit log linked to that
decision, flips the submission's status to **`under_review`**, and returns confirmation. (Automated
re-classification is intentionally *not* done — a human reviewer is the point.)

## Detection Signals

At least two **distinct** signals — one semantic, one structural — so the combination is more
informative than either alone.

### Signal 1 — LLM semantic classification (Groq `llama-3.3-70b-versatile`)

- **What it measures:** holistic semantic and stylistic coherence — does the text *read* as written
  by a person? Picks up generic phrasing, even hedging, over-smooth transitions, "balanced" structure,
  and absence of idiosyncratic voice.
- **Why it differs (human vs AI):** default LLM prose tends to be fluent-but-generic, evenly hedged,
  and "safe"; human writing carries voice, surprising word choices, uneven emphasis, and risk.
- **Blind spots (what it can't capture):**
  - Fooled by AI prompted to imitate a specific human voice, and by *bland human writing* (student
    essays, corporate copy) — a real **false-positive** risk.
  - Non-deterministic: same text can yield different scores; the model can be confidently wrong.
  - Genre-sensitive (poetry vs. blog vs. technical) and subject to model/prompt drift.
  - Weak on very short texts (little to judge).
  - A black box — its rationale isn't always trustworthy.

### Signal 2 — Stylometric heuristics (pure Python, no external libs)

- **What it measures:** statistical structure of the text:
  - **Sentence-length variance ("burstiness")** — variation across sentence lengths.
  - **Type–token ratio (lexical diversity)** — unique words ÷ total words.
  - **Punctuation density / variety** — commas, dashes, semicolons per 100 words.
- **Why it differs (human vs AI):** default-temperature AI text is uniform and "smooth" — low sentence
  variance, middling lexical diversity, even punctuation. Human writing is **bursty** and uneven.
- **Blind spots (what it can't capture):**
  - Very sensitive to length — short texts give unstable, meaningless stats.
  - Genre-breaking (poetry, lists, dialogue, technical writing) defeats the assumptions.
  - Trivially gamed (a human who writes uniformly; AI told to "vary your sentence length").
  - **No understanding of meaning** — can't catch AI text that happens to be statistically bursty.
  - Thresholds are hand-tuned heuristics, not learned/calibrated.

**Why these two are independent:** Signal 1 is about *meaning and voice*; Signal 2 is about *measurable
form*. Their blind spots barely overlap — e.g., bland-but-bursty human writing fools each in opposite
directions, which is exactly the disagreement the fusion step uses to say "uncertain" instead of guessing.

## Confidence Scoring

**Decide what the number means to a user first** (per the hint), then compute it.

- We track a single **`ai_likelihood` ∈ [0,1]** (0 = clearly human, 1 = clearly AI) and a separate
  **`confidence` ∈ [0,1]** = *how decisive / trustworthy this verdict is*.
- **What 0.5 means:** "we genuinely can't tell." A `confidence` near 0.5 (or an `ai_likelihood` near
  0.5) must produce the **uncertain** label — never a confident accusation. 0.51 and 0.95 must land in
  visibly different labels; that's the whole point.
- **Fusion approach (default, adjustable):**
  - `combined = 0.6 * s1_llm + 0.4 * s2_stylometry` — LLM weighted higher because the semantic signal
    is the more robust of the two; stylometry is supporting evidence.
  - **Agreement adjustment:** the more the two signals disagree, the lower the confidence. Confidence
    is high only when both signals point the same way *and* the combined score is near an extreme.
- **False-positive asymmetry (encoded here):** the bar to *assert AI* is deliberately higher than the
  bar to say *human*.
  - "Likely AI-generated" only when `combined ≥ 0.75` **and** both signals agree → high confidence.
  - "Likely human-written" when `combined ≤ 0.35`.
  - Everything in between → **uncertain**. (The uncertain band is wider on the AI side on purpose.)
- **How we'll test the score is meaningful (Milestone 2/4):** run a small labeled set — known
  human pieces, known AI pieces, and deliberately ambiguous ones — and check that (a) clear cases get
  high confidence, (b) ambiguous/short/genre-odd cases fall into "uncertain," and (c) tweaking one
  signal moves the score in the expected direction.

## Transparency Labels (3 variants)

Reader-facing, plain language, confidence made meaningful to a non-technical reader. Wording is
cautious by design — even the "AI" label says *estimate*, never an accusation. **Draft text** (final
verbatim copy will be mirrored into the README):

| Variant               | Selected when                              | Draft label text |
|-----------------------|--------------------------------------------|------------------|
| High-confidence human | `combined ≤ 0.35` & signals agree          | ✍️ **Likely written by a person.** Our automated check found strong signs of human authorship. *(Confidence: High)* |
| High-confidence AI    | `combined ≥ 0.75` & signals agree          | 🤖 **Likely AI-generated.** Our automated check found strong signs of AI assistance. This is an estimate, not a certainty — the creator can appeal. *(Confidence: High)* |
| Uncertain             | anything else (incl. signal disagreement)  | ❓ **Origin unclear.** Our automated check couldn't reliably tell whether a person or AI wrote this, so we're not making a claim either way. *(Confidence: Low)* |

## Appeals Workflow

- **Input:** `POST /appeal` with `submission_id` and the creator's `reasoning` (free text).
- **Steps:** look up the original decision → insert an **appeal row** into the audit log, linked to
  that decision and storing the reasoning → set the submission's `status` to **`under_review`**.
- **No automated re-classification** — the appeal flags it for a human; that's the design intent.
- **Output:** `{ appeal_id, submission_id, status: "under_review" }`.

## False-Positive Walkthrough (the scenario that must work)

**Scenario:** A poet submits spare, minimalist free verse — short, evenly-shaped lines.

1. **Signal 2 (stylometry)** sees low sentence-length variance and modest lexical diversity →
   leans **AI** (~0.7). This is the classic stylometric false positive.
2. **Signal 1 (LLM)** reads genuine craft, voice, and intent → leans **human** (~0.25).
3. **Fusion:** the two signals *strongly disagree*. The agreement adjustment **drops confidence**, and
   the asymmetric threshold refuses to assert "AI" without agreement → verdict = **uncertain**.
4. **Label:** the reader sees the **"Origin unclear … not making a claim either way"** label — *not*
   an AI accusation. The asymmetry did its job.
5. **Appeal:** if the poet still objects, they `POST /appeal` with their reasoning ("this is my
   original free verse, here's my draft history"). The status flips to `under_review`, the appeal is
   logged beside the original decision, and a human can follow up.

This is why disagreement → uncertain and why the AI bar is high: it converts a likely false accusation
into an honest "we don't know," with a clear path to contest.

## API Surface (the contract)

| Method | Path                 | Accepts                                              | Returns |
|--------|----------------------|------------------------------------------------------|---------|
| POST   | `/submit`            | `{ content: str, creator_id?: str, title?: str }`    | `{ submission_id, verdict, ai_likelihood, confidence, label: {variant, text}, signals: [{name, score, detail}], created_at }` |
| POST   | `/appeal`            | `{ submission_id: str, reasoning: str }`             | `{ appeal_id, submission_id, status: "under_review", logged_at }` |
| GET    | `/log`               | — (optional `?limit=`)                               | `[ { id, type: "decision"|"appeal", ... } ]` structured audit entries |
| GET    | `/submission/<id>`   | — (optional, convenience)                            | the stored decision for one submission |
| GET    | `/health`            | —                                                    | `{ status: "ok" }` (optional) |

**Required:** `/submit`, `/appeal`, `/log`. `/submission/<id>` and `/health` are optional conveniences.
